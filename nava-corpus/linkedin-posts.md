## Post 4 — Apr 13, 2026 — The Decision / Administrator

The administrators could call this a staffing problem.

They could blame the travel agencies, the gig economy, or the nurses who left for better pay elsewhere.

But that would mean missing the thing that's actually happening.

A nurse reached week eleven of her contract. She had learned which physicians prefer morning rounds, which patients need extra time, and which charge nurse to call when a rapid response gets called. She had survived the orientation phase on the unit and was finally operating at full capacity.

Week thirteen arrived, and she left.

The reason is not mystery. The system was never built to keep her.

Agencies profit when nurses move. Every contract is a billable hour. Every new placement is new revenue. A nurse who knows your unit is a nurse who's harder to place. The learning curve is a product line.

The administrators who are watching this happen through the glass are not failing. They are watching a machine operate exactly as designed.

Nava was built differently. We structure around permanence from the first conversation:
 • Truth at placement,
 • Follow-up after arrival,
 • A direct line when things go wrong.

Because we don't make money on turnover, we built a model that ends the 13-week reset.

## Post 4 — Apr 13, 2026 — The Decision / Nurse

She was six weeks from the end of her contract and already dreading it.

Not because the unit was hard. Not because the patients were different. Because she knew that in six weeks the unit would feel familiar, the physicians would know her name, the patients she'd been caring for would have trusting faces — and she would be gone.

Transferred to somewhere she'd never been. Starting over with a new team, new workflows, new routines.

The 13-week reset is sold as a feature of the travel model. In reality, it's a mechanism for keeping nurses in a constant state of transition — too new to demand better conditions, too unfamiliar to be irreplaceable.

The ones who almost stayed.

They are the nurses who learned your unit well enough to know where the supply cabinet actually is, which charge nurse actually helps, which physicians actually listen.

They left anyway. Not because they wanted to. Because the model moves them before they can become expensive.

Nava was built to be different. We structure around permanence from the first conversation:
 • Truth at placement,
 • Follow-up after arrival,
 • Relationships that outlast the contract cycle.

We don't profit on turnover. We built a model that ends the 13-week reset.

## Post 3 — Apr 9, 2026 — Continuity / Merry-Go-Rounds

A travelling nurse reaches week seven of their contract and finally understands the culture and where the best karaoke in town is. Week nine, they've started anticipating which physicians prefer which workflows.

Then week thirteen arrives, and they leave.

𝗧𝗵𝗶𝘀 𝗵𝗮𝗽𝗽𝗲𝗻𝘀 𝗹𝗶𝗸𝗲 𝗰𝗹𝗼𝗰𝗸𝘄𝗼𝗿𝗸, but not because nurses want to leave. Some negotiate to come back. Some choose their next contract carefully. But many leave because this is how to make a living in this industry.

The 13-week reset is a structural feature of the travel staffing model. Agencies profit on volume: new placements, new contracts, new orientation gaps. A nurse who knows your unit intimately is a nurse who's harder to place. Familiarity breeds comfort, and places unknown come with uncertainty and social anxiety. So the system moves them before they feel at home.

𝗬𝗼𝘂𝗿 𝘂𝗻𝗶𝘁 𝗽𝗮𝘆𝘀 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗽𝗿𝗶𝗰𝗲: hand-off errors, orientation costs, the institutional knowledge that never accumulates because it keeps walking out the door.

We built Nava differently. Continuity requires someone who stays, so we structure around that from day one:
 • Truth at placement,
 • Follow-up after arrival,
 • Relationships that outlast the contract cycle.
