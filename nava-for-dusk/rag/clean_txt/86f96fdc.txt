2 | What Are the Main Healthcare Staffing Models in 2025?
Healthcare staffing models outline how a facility organizes its workforce to achieve patient care and operational objectives. In practice, these models strike a balance between full-time employment, contingent labor, and hybrid partnerships to ensure stability without overspending. The
Symplr Healthcare Trends Report 2025
notes that flexible frameworks outperform rigid models by adapting to census changes and seasonal patient surges while preserving quality standards.
Each approach offers trade-offs. Permanent teams foster culture and continuity but require long-term investment. Contingent models provide agility but risk inconsistency. Partnership-based hybrids merge both leveraging external expertise while maintaining institutional oversight.
Key Insight:
The most resilient facilities in 2025 employ multiple staffing models simultaneously, aligning flexibility with predictable quality controls.

Permanent staffing remains the foundation of operational and clinical stability. The

found hospitals that implemented structured engagement programs reduced turnover by 37 percent. Permanent models are essential in care environments that rely on deep familiarity with patient populations, such as long-term care and specialty hospitals.

(HFMA)

2.1 | Temporary and Per-Diem Staffing
Temporary staffing remains indispensable for covering rapid vacancies, census surges, and specialized needs. According to HFMA’s labor cost analysis, over-reliance on short-term staff can increase total labor costs by up to 30 percent once orientation and credentialing are included. The
AHA Workforce Scan
reinforces that facilities combining contingent workers with a consistent core workforce maintain both care continuity and financial control.
Short-term or per-diem models work best when applied deliberately for seasonal relief, maternity leaves, or temporary expansions rather than as a standing replacement for permanent roles. Proper onboarding, consistent communication, and post-assignment evaluation are essential to prevent skill dilution.

2.5 | Locum Tenens and Contingent Professionals
Contingent clinicians deliver essential coverage for rural or specialty settings but cost substantially more than core staff. The American Hospital Association warns that continuous reliance on locum labor elevates total labor spend by up to 40 percent when housing, stipends, and onboarding are included. Strategic deployment within defined periods preserves flexibility without eroding margins.
Key Insight:
Contingent staffing should fill defined, temporary needs, and not serve as a standing workforce model.

4 | What Role Does Compliance and Credentialing Play?
Every staffing model depends on reliable credentialing. The Joint Commission mandates rigorous verification of licensure, training, and competency for all healthcare providers. Facilities integrating automated credential management platforms cut administrative processing time by nearly 40 percent, according to HFMA benchmarks. Streamlined compliance procedures not only reduce legal risk but also enhance readiness for audits and renewals.
Organizations reporting the greatest difficulty filling leadership positions also note delays in onboarding, credentialing, and alignment with organizational culture — as highlighted in
Recruiting Hard to Fill Leadership Roles in Healthcare
.
These gaps emphasise that a staffing-model must address not only quantity but also the readiness of leadership talent.
Key Insight:
Centralized digital credentialing systems improve speed, reduce risk, and serve as the backbone of scalable staffing operations.
5 | What’s Next for Workforce Planning in Healthcare?
As workforce shortages persist, technology will increasingly shape staffing strategy. Predictive analytics and AI-driven scheduling tools now enable administrators to forecast vacancies, reduce overtime, and align staffing with patient volume in real time. The McKinsey Health Institute projects that AI-enabled workforce management could cut clinical vacancy rates by 15 percent by 2030. Facilities that combine these technologies with long-term partnership models will gain a measurable advantage in stability and retention.
For healthcare executives, the future lies in integration unifying technology, partnerships, and internal development into a single staffing ecosystem. The next era of healthcare staffing will prioritize sustainability, adaptability, and outcomes over short-term fixes.
