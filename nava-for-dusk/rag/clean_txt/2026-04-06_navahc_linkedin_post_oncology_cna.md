Title: Eight patients. One CNA. In a rehab unit, that would be a typical Tuesday, but in an oncology unit? That's a crisis.
Author: Nava Healthcare Recruitment (Corporate)
Date: 2026-04-06
Type: LinkedIn corporate post
Persona: Nava Corporate

Eight patients. One CNA. In a rehab unit, that would be a typical Tuesday, but in an oncology unit? That's a crisis.

That happened in Wichita and still does in other cities across the US. But the story here isn't Wichita, it's the $39 billion industry built around making sure these acuity gaps keep happening.

Travel nursing is now a permanent feature of American healthcare, not a crisis response. The market hit $39 billion last year. It's projected to reach $72 billion by 2033. That growth isn't a sign the system is solving its staffing problem, but a symptom the system is more dependent on the thing causing the problem.

Here's the cycle: hospitals pay 32-65% agency markups. Permanent nurses leave because travelers earn 25% more for the same work. Hospitals can't afford the raises that might bring them back. So they travel more. The cycle feeds itself.

The nurse on that oncology floor in Wichita? She's the output of a system that decided a $39 billion temporary labor market was easier than fixing why nurses keep leaving.

We're spending our way into a hole we're still digging.

This particular story starts in Wichita, but it can end, if we want it to, with structural change. Which is why we built NurseSphere.
