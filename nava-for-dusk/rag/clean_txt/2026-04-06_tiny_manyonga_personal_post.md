When I was leading SDR teams, growth was the only metric that moved the needle. We lived for the "up and to the right" trajectory. If revenue was climbing, everyone was happy. Growth, by definition, was the goal.

But since moving into healthcare strategy at Nava, I've had to reconsider what growth actually signals. In a sales funnel, growth is a victory. In an oncology ward, it is a diagnosis.

The travel nursing market is usually quoted at $39 billion. But that number doesn't tell you anything without the frame. In 2019, it was a $6.5 billion niche. By 2022, it hit $42.7 billion. Today, even after the post-pandemic correction, it is sitting at $25 billion. That is still nearly four times the pre-pandemic level.

Here is the part that should stop a data person in their tracks: travel nurse wages only rose about 20% over the same period. If the market is four times larger, but the price of labor only went up by a fifth, the math is not about inflation. It is about structure. We have fundamentally shifted from a permanent clinical workforce to a contingent one. We are not paying more per nurse. We are paying a markup to replace the permanent foundation with temporary labor, again and again.

This is not a US problem. Japan is already super-ageing. The UK is strained. By 2030, one in six people globally will be over 60. In the US, the 65+ population will drive half of all healthcare spending within this decade. We are sleep-walking into a permanent floor of high-acuity demand while locking capital into an arbitrage cycle that prizes the transaction over the retention.

When I see oncology units struggling with 8:1 ratios, I do not see a staffing shortage. I see a failure of resource allocation. We are spending $25 billion a year treating a chronic, thirty-year demographic shift with a transactional Band-Aid.

And the market is projected to reach $72 billion by 2033.

This is why my role at Nava feels less like recruitment and more like structural engineering.

At what point do we stop celebrating market growth and start diagnosing systemic insolvency? I would love to hear from the data-driven leaders in my network. Is this growth, or are we just subsidising a hole we are still digging?
