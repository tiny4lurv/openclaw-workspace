## The Problem This Article Addresses

Healthcare organizations are mistaking **tool access for strategy**. Job boards promised easier hiring — more resumes, wider reach, surplus of applicants — but for most systems running on limited infrastructure, they've delivered churn, disengagement, and staffing mismatches that create more problems than they solve. The real failure isn't the tools themselves; it's the conceptual error of relying on volume-optimized platforms as the *primary engine* of healthcare hiring.

## The Industry Practice Being Called Out

**Passive, post-and-pray job board strategy** — posting generic ads, waiting for submissions, filtering poorly, and treating the resulting resume flood as progress. The article identifies three endemic problems:
1. **Résumé Overload:** Job boards deliver blind submissions, not vetted candidates — most unvetted for licenses, preferences, or geographic accuracy
2. **Filter Failures:** Shift preferences, behavioral alignment, travel flexibility get buried or untracked — recruiters waste hours chasing shadows
3. **The Ghosting Cycle:** Click-to-apply reduces candidate commitment — nearly half of all applicants disengage after applying, citing lack of engagement

The deeper issue: job boards were built for **high-turnover, low-specialization roles** — not for clinical care where a bad hire = burnout, liability, and worse patient outcomes.

## Stakeholder Pain Points

**Healthcare Recruiters:**
- Hidden time tax: hours per day sifting through unvetted resumes
- Recruiter burnout starts in the inbox, not on the floor
- "Click, apply, ghost. Repeat." — cycle that wastes hours per role
- Dashboards look full but placements fall apart after two months

**Hiring Managers / Clinical Leaders:**
- Feedback delays and mid-stream criteria changes stall processes
- Poor communication between recruiters and clinical teams is a top delay reason
- Credentialing lag (weeks to months for multi-state licenses) kills momentum

**Candidates:**
- Job posts omit acuity levels, shift intensity, unit culture — candidates can't accurately self-assess
- "Shift Shock" (referenced) — mismatched acuity levels and unwanted float assignments drive early attrition
- Low-commitment application process leads to disengagement

## Nava's Response

Job boards are not inherently useless — but they require **recruiter-level access, proactive search, and consistent follow-up** to generate real value. Nava uses:
- Job boards as a *discovery tool* (finding a name) not a decision engine
- Personalized outreach at scale — messaging dozens or hundreds of candidates per single role
- Deep clinical insight combined with relationship-building to confirm behavioral fit, emotional readiness, and long-term alignment
- Post-placement accountability: support well past onboarding, not just handoffs and hope

The article is explicit: "We do not dismiss job boards but neither do we do volume for volume's sake. We do alignment built on clinical insight, deep vetting, and long-term fit."

## What I Now Understand That I Didn't Before

1. Job board failure isn't primarily a tool problem — it's a *design* problem. The tools work for what they were built for (high-turnover, low-specialization roles). Healthcare hiring is architecturally different: fit, acuity alignment, team culture, and behavioral resilience matter more than credential keywords. Using volume tools for fit-focused hiring is a category mismatch, not a tool failure.
2. The "hidden time tax" on recruiters is a real cost that often goes unmeasured. If your recruiting team spends 3 hours/day on fruitless resume review, that's a significant dollar amount that's invisible on any spreadsheet until you look for it.
