## The Problem This Article Addresses

Healthcare leaders are using the wrong scorecard to evaluate staffing agencies — and it's costing them millions. The industry's default metrics (time-to-fill and cost-per-hire) are intuitively appealing but fundamentally misaligned with the actual goal: building a stable, high-quality clinical workforce. Leaders think they're measuring agency success, but they're actually incentivizing speed and low upfront fees while ignoring the catastrophic downstream cost of clinician turnover.

## The Industry Practice Being Called Out

**Transactional scorecard metrics** — specifically "time-to-fill" and "cost-per-hire" — as the dominant framework for evaluating healthcare staffing agencies. The industry has normalized these metrics so thoroughly that 43% of clients cite time-to-fill as the most important metric. Nava calls this the "speed-over-quality trap" that structurally incentivizes agencies to push misaligned candidates forward and fill immediate gaps while fueling the long-term turnover crisis.

## Nava's Response

Nava proposes replacing the transactional scorecard with a **"Quality of Hire (QoH) Scorecard"** — a strategic framework built on three layers:

1. **Foundational: Role Brief adherence** — Score the agency's "fit-to-brief" ratio; did they vet candidates against a co-created role brief or just push resumes?
2. **Core: Long-term retention metrics** — Track 90-day AND 1-year clinician retention rates, not just placement speed
3. **Leading indicator: Day Zero candidate experience** — Measure candidate feedback scores, time-to-credentialing, and Day One readiness

Alongside the scorecard, Nava recommends:
- **Quarterly Business Reviews (QBRs)** — a structured, data-driven forum replacing ad-hoc emails and panicked calls
- **Retention-based fee structure** — Nava specifically calls out their own model: clients pay 30 days after floor hire (60 days for management), eliminating upfront fees and making Nava's financial success directly tied to retention

## What I Now Understand That I Didn't Before

1. The 90-day guarantee isn't just inadequate — it's actively exploitative. It lets agencies collect full fees on placements that fail fast (57% of all turnover happens in 90 days) while the facility absorbs the real costs of disruption, training waste, and patient care risk.

2. The $61,000 RN turnover figure is just the starting point. The article references "1.3x to 5x that individual's annual salary" when total impact is calculated — including lost productivity, team disruption, and patient care impacts. This makes the agency's $14,000–$23,400 fee look like a rounding error against potential $150,000–$400,000+ total losses per bad hire for specialized roles.

3. A 3:1 interview-to-placement ratio is the benchmark for a high-performing agency. This means for every 3 candidates presented for interviews, the agency delivers 1 successful placement — indicating rigorous pre-screening. A 500:1 ratio (common on job boards) is described as proof of a failed screening process, not a successful talent funnel.
