## The Problem This Article Addresses

Healthcare leaders are stuck on a **false binary**: EB-3 versus TN, as if these are competing options on the same menu. This binary framing leads to paralysis or sub-optimal decisions — facilities choose one pathway and later discover the other would have better served a different position, or they avoid international hiring entirely because the choice feels irreversible. The real problem is that leaders have never seen all five strategic variables laid out simultaneously, so they default to travel nurses (the path of least resistance) even when EB-3 or TN would be structurally superior.

## The Industry Practice Being Called Out

The **procurement-fee lens** that CFOs use to evaluate international recruitment — comparing only the fixed fees of TN ($7,000–$15,000) or EB-3 ($25,000–$35,000) against each other, while ignoring the **vacancy cost math**: the $40,000–$80,000 in accumulated costs and lost revenue from a single open requisition left unfilled for six months. This narrow comparison makes TN look expensive and EB-3 look slow, when the actual question is which pathway serves which position on what timeline.

The article also calls out the **facilities that abandon EB-3 halfway through** — those that start the process, lose patience, and switch back to travel nurses, thereby absorbing the sunk cost of EB-3 *plus* the ongoing agency premium with nothing to show for either investment. This "worst of both worlds" outcome is treated as a structural risk of not committing fully to the chosen pathway.

Finally, the article takes on the **outdated information problem**: facilities still treating EB-3 as uniformly slow, unaware that the 2026 visa bulletin changes (EB-3 ROW now "Current") have dramatically accelerated approvals for Rest of World candidates.

## Stakeholder Pain Points

**CFO:**
- Comparing TN and EB-3 fees in isolation without factoring vacancy costs leads to false economies; an open req costs $40K–$80K over six months, dwarfing the fee delta
- Facilities that abandon EB-3 halfway absorb sunk costs plus ongoing agency premiums — the worst financial outcome in the international hiring spectrum

**HR Director / Workforce Planner:**
- No framework for evaluating which pathway serves which position — the five variables (vacancy urgency, budget reality, commitment horizon, candidate origin, retention objective) are presented but rarely all at once
- The "complexity barrier" of EB-3 (I-140, PERM, consular processing) has historically made TN the default even when permanence was the actual objective

**Nursing Leadership / CNO:**
- When a facility uses TN as a permanent solution to a structural problem, they replicate the travel nurse dependency with a different label
- Non-immigrant intent structure of TN means nurses must maintain intent to return home — a structural ceiling on retention that the article calls out explicitly

**Immigration / Compliance Team:**
- India-born EB-3 candidates present a frank risk: priority dates stuck at 2012–2013 mean the queue is not moving in any practical sense; the article cites a case where 3 of 4 candidates withdrew over four years
- Premium processing as an option ($2,965) feels contextually strange for roles that are "a matter of life and death for US citizens," but the math makes it a rounding error against vacancy costs

## Nava's Response

**The parallel track strategy**: running EB-3 and TN simultaneously within the same health system, each serving different strategic objectives. TN fills the urgent gaps (positions needing coverage within 6 months) while EB-3 builds the permanent staffing base over a 3+ year horizon. This is framed not as twice the work but as a more sophisticated staffing infrastructure.

Nava runs both tracks operationally — TN pipeline for candidates who qualify under USMCA, EB-3 pipeline for candidates where permanence is the objective. The article positions this dual capability as Nava's fundamental differentiator: the competitor to the false binary is not "choose the right one" but "stop pretending only one track exists."

## What I Now Understand That I Didn't Before

1. **Geography is the dominant variable in EB-3 timing** — not process efficiency, not candidate quality, not facility diligence. A Philippines-born candidate moves through the queue faster than an India-born candidate purely because of demand and backlog. This means EB-3 counseling must begin with country-of-birth analysis, not just timeline estimates.
2. **The "Dates for Filing" → "Current" shift in early 2026 is operationally significant** — candidates who were mathematically projected to arrive in late 2027 are now on track for 2026. This changes the vacancy cost calculation entirely for facilities with ROW candidates already in process.
3. **The TN retention ceiling is structural, not cultural** — it's not that TN nurses don't want to stay; it's that their legal status requires maintenance of non-immigrant intent, which by definition creates an incentive structure pointed toward departure. EB-3 permanence aligns legal status with professional future.
4. **The Healthcare Workforce Resilience Act recapture mechanism is active and real** — 25,000 nursing slots and 15,000 physician slots from unused approved petitions represent a secondary pathway that attentive workforce planners should monitor against the standard queue.
