## The Problem This Article Addresses

Healthcare compliance vetting in staffing is broken at a structural level — not because facilities don't try, but because the industry standard "compliance-as-checklist" model is fundamentally aimed at the wrong target. The real risk isn't database errors (expired licenses, OIG exclusions) — it's the "hidden flaw": behavioral incompetence, professional negligence, or outright fraud that database checks are structurally incapable of finding. The specific structural failure: transactional agencies are economically incentivized to prioritize speed over quality, legally insulated from their own vetting failures, and therefore rationally "compliance-agnostic" — passing the "hot potato" of liability to hospitals. Hospitals retain 100% of legal and financial risk while paying premium rates for a vetting process that failed to protect them.

## The Industry Practice Being Called Out

**"Compliance-as-Checklist"** — the dominant model in healthcare staffing that:
1. Verifies a credential's *existence* in a database (a "snapshot" that's obsolete the moment it's completed)
2. Stops low-consequence database mismatches while being "completely blind to the rare but high-consequence, catastrophic threat of the fraudulent or dangerous provider"
3. Treats the signed contract as the end of agency responsibility — leaving hospitals 100% exposed to *future* risk (new sanctions, expiring licenses, behavioral issues)

**The "Transactional Agency" model** — agencies whose primary economic incentive is speed (fill the role, collect the fee) and who rationally do the bare minimum compliance work because:
- Compliance is a "direct barrier" to their revenue incentive
- They have no legal consequence for vetting failures (liability falls 100% on the hospital)
- No economic incentive to be thorough

## Stakeholder Pain Points

**[Hospital Administrators / Risk Managers]:**
- Retain 100% of legal and financial liability for credentialing while paying premium rates for vetting that may be functionally inadequate
- "False and dangerous sense of security" from snapshot compliance checks — unaware of the window of risk between database verification and actual clinician fitness
- "Undesirable choice" between slow meticulous vetting (leaving units short-staffed) or minimum compliance (accepting the 1-in-100 high-impact risk)
- Under the "lent employee" doctrine, the hospital is always the liable employer — agency agreements don't protect them

**[Compliance Officers]:**
- The Joint Commission (TJC) standards require hospitals to perform their own credentialing and background checks — non-delegable
- Database-only checks are "structurally blind" to behavioral red flags: "Is this clinician diligent or just certified?", "Are they resilient or resistant?", "Are they a collaborator or a source of ripple effect?"
- No mechanism to catch credentials that are facially valid but entirely fraudulent (Operation Nightingale proof)

**[CNOs / Unit Managers]:**
- Bear 100% of the future risk after contract is signed — new license sanctions, behavioral issues, performance problems fall on unit managers
- Must orient, supervise, and correct errors of agency staff "unfamiliar with protocols or who demonstrate a lack of professionalism"
- "Primary driver of burnout, resentment, and a toxic work environment" — adds uncompensated workload
- Permanent staff burnout directly accelerates the very turnover the agency "solution" was meant to solve

**[Finance / Billing]:**
- OIG exclusion violations carry fines up to $20,000 per claim item — catastrophic exposure from a single bad hire
- Average negligent hiring settlement: $1 million, with 75% of cases resulting in loss for the employer

## Nava's Response

Nava's **"Assurance Model"** — a relationship-first compliance framework with three core structural components:

1. **Strategic Focus on Gaps Databases Miss**: Nava efficiently handles "table stakes" compliance (active licenses, certifications) as pre-vetting, freeing resources to focus on the "hidden flaws" databases can't see — behavioral competencies, professional diligence patterns, cultural fit

2. **"Human Intelligence" Model**: Not a database query but "lasting, ongoing relationships with both clinicians and a deep network of healthcare leaders" — the sole mechanism that finds what databases cannot. Answers questions like: "Is the clinician diligent or just certified?", "Are they resilient or resistant?", "Are they a collaborator or a source of ripple effect?"

3. **Aligned Incentives**: Billing only after a clinician has been successful on the job for 30-60 days — structurally realigns incentives away from speed toward quality. Gives the hospital "time to experience the hidden flaws (or strengths) in a real-world setting"
