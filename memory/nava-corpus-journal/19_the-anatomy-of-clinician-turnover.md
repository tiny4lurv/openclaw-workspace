## Strategic Summary

**What this article is really about:** Early clinician turnover isn't monolithic — it falls into three distinct, diagnosable buckets: Uncontrollable (life events), Transactional (preventable mismatch), and Systemic (bureaucratic rigidity). Misdiagnosing which bucket a resignation falls into guarantees the wrong solution. The article introduces Nava's intervention model: a retention diagnostician that intervenes to save hires that policy would destroy.

**Core argument arc:**

1. **The Zero-ROI Event:** Early resignation = sunk cost with zero return. Facility pays recruitment, onboarding, training, preceptor time. Receives insufficient productive output. $61,110 per RN (2024, 8.6% increase YoY).

2. **The Three Buckets:**

   **Bucket 1 — Uncontrollable (Life Happens):**
   - External life events outside facility's control (spouse transferred, parent requires care, relocation)
   - Only truly "unpreventable" exits
   - BUT: 95.4% of early separations are VOLUNTARY — most leaders overestimate this bucket
   - Trap: mislabeling preventable mismatch as unavoidable to avoid internal accountability

   **Bucket 2 — Transactional (The Mismatch):**
   - "Shift Shock" — 72% of new hires realize job reality contradicts description within days
   - Mail-sorting logic: keywords + valid license + pulse
   - Agencies incentivized for speed over "retention-minded matching"
   - The ONLY truly preventable bucket at the sourcing phase
   - Cannot be rescued post-hire; must be prevented at vetting

   **Bucket 3 — Systemic (The Guardrail Trap):**
   - Candidate WANTS to stay, facility NEEDS them to stay, but rigid policy forces separation
   - Resource rigidity: fixed ratios, mandatory overtime, denied PTO for "operational needs"
   - "Guardrail" designed for fairness becomes weapon that drives talent away
   - Entirely self-inflicted — star employees lost over minor policy infractions

3. **The Diagnostic Gap:** 78% of executives think staff leave due to lack of flexibility. 75% of nurses cite pay and unsafe staffing. Leaders and nurses are diagnosing different problems → ineffective retention strategies.

4. **Nava's Intervention Model:**
   - Acts as retention diagnostician, not just recruiter
   - **Uncontrollable:** Validates life event, de-escalates client anger, preserves relationship for potential re-hire
   - **Transactional:** Prevents via rigorous front-end behavioral vetting — "we don't have rescue stories for this bucket because we engineered it out"
   - **Systemic:** Intervenes at executive level when policy threatens critical hire — challenges financial logic of rigidity

5. **Real-World Cases:**
   - RN MDS Coordinator quit after 1 month — furious client. Truth: urgent family move to distant state. Nava validated exit, cooled relationship, preserved re-hire possibility.
   - CMA in SC: star employee, loved by client, quitting over 2.5 hours for son's graduation. Policy denied PTO for new hires. Nava called CFO, challenged financial logic, policy waived, hire saved.
